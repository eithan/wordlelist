Most Wordle advice is vibes. This guide is arithmetic: every claim below is computed from the complete archive of real Wordle answers. Six habits, in the order they'll save your streak.

Habit 1

Open With a Proven Word — Then Stop Thinking About It

Your first guess is the only one you can optimize in advance, so optimize it once and move on. Against every real answer ever played, STARE hits an average of 1.79 letters per game with the best green rate in its tier.

STARE

Prefer alternatives? LATER, ALERT and IRATE edge it slightly on raw coverage. The one thing the data is unambiguous about: vowel-dump openers like ADIEU underperform badly (#217 of all starters). The full ranking is on our best starting words page.

Habit 2

Make Your Second Guess Do Real Work

The most common intermediate-player mistake is chasing one yellow letter with guess two. Unless your opener landed 3+ hits, a second guess with five completely new letters almost always tells you more. The strongest fixed pair we've measured is LATER → SONIC: together they touch at least one letter in 99.3% of all real answers, and three or more letters in 76%.

SONIC

From guess three onward, switch modes: now you solve, using everything you've learned.

Habit 3

Think in Frequencies, Not Hunches

When choosing between candidate words, prefer the one built from common letters and common positions. The workhorse facts, from all 1,826 answers: E appears in 47% of answers and ends nearly one in five; A (40%) and R (36%) are next; 15% of answers start with S; and Y — mediocre overall — is secretly the second-best final letter (14%). Meanwhile J, Q, Z and X combined appear in under 7% of answers: never spend a scarce guess on them without evidence. More of these on the stats & facts page.

Habit 4

Respect the Repeated Letter

31% of all real answers contain a repeated letter. Almost one game in three. If you never consider doubles, you will regularly find yourself with four greens and no idea why nothing fits.

The tell: when several yellows/greens are placed but every candidate you try feels "used up," start doubling. LL, OO and EE are the most common pairs, and 13 answers have even used a letter three times (ERROR, GEESE, PUPPY…).

Habit 5

Never Guess a Plural

Only 1.5% of answers end in S — and essentially none of those are simple plurals (they're words like CHAOS and FOCUS). An S in the fifth slot on a hunch is close to a wasted guess. The same caution applies to -ED and -ER verb forms padded out to five letters.

Habit 6

Escape Word-Family Traps With One Probe

Four greens and a blank first letter is the classic streak-killer. Land on _ATCH and there are still seven real possibilities: BATCH, CATCH, HATCH, LATCH, MATCH, PATCH, WATCH — more remaining candidates than remaining guesses.

Instead of testing initials one at a time, spend one guess on a word packed with candidate first letters. From _ATCH, guessing something like CLAMP tests C, L, M and P simultaneously — one guess eliminates four branches. (On hard mode this is illegal, which is exactly why hard-mode players should bias toward green-heavy openers like SLATE from day one.)

-IGHT, -OUND and -OWER are the other big families to watch for.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the best strategy for Wordle?

Open with a proven high-coverage word like STARE, use guess two to test five new letters, prefer common letters and positions, and stay alert for repeated letters — they appear in 31% of all real answers.

Is there a trick to winning Wordle every time?

No guaranteed trick exists, but disciplined play makes losing rare — strong openers, information-dense second guesses, and avoiding known traps like plurals. Careful players solve well over 95% of puzzles.

What is a good average number of guesses?

Around 4.0 is solid for regular players; consistently under 3.7 is genuinely strong. The habits above are worth roughly half a guess per game for most people.

Should I use a different starting word every day?

Statistically it barely matters — but a fixed, proven opener removes a decision and guarantees consistent information. See the full starter rankings.
